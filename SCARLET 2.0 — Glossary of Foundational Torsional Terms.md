SCARLET 2.0 — Glossary of Torsional Properties

Note on Terminology and Proper Naming

Scarlet 2.0 intentionally uses proper physical naming conventions for foundational structures rather than relying solely on inherited philosophical terminology from standard theoretical physics.

Many conventional terms in modern physics—such as “vacuum,” “dark energy,” “field,” or even “empty space”—are historically useful, but often describe observed behavior rather than the underlying physical structure responsible for that behavior. These terms are frequently phenomenological labels rather than material definitions.

The goal of Scarlet is to move from descriptive language toward constitutive language.

Rather than treating spacetime as an abstract geometric background, Scarlet treats it as a structured torsion-capable medium with measurable stiffness, fatigue thresholds, recovery dynamics, and discrete anchoring scales. At the deepest level, this structure is organized through two constitutive sectors:

Warp — the longitudinal alignment structure governing coherence, anchoring, and geometric ordering
Weft — the transverse torsional response governing propagation, displacement, and elastic restoration
These are not metaphorical names. They are operational descriptors of how the spacetime fabric organizes and transmits physical structure.

In such a framework, naming conventions must reflect physical function rather than historical convention.

Terms such as:

Bedrock Horizon Scale
Warp Field
Weft Field
Torsional Multiplicity
Geometric Drag
Fatigue Coefficient
Torsional Phase Field
are therefore introduced not as philosophical reinterpretations, but as operational names for specific measurable properties of the spacetime fabric.

These names are chosen to clarify:

what the quantity physically represents,
how it behaves dynamically, and
how it connects across scales from microscopic geometry to cosmological observables.
This follows the same scientific tradition by which terms such as “color charge,” “spin,” “inflation,” or “dark matter” became accepted once the underlying structure justified the language.

Scarlet does not introduce new terminology for novelty; it introduces terminology where standard language becomes structurally incomplete.

The purpose is precision, not rebranding.

If a quantity behaves as stiffness, it is named stiffness.
If a quantity behaves as fatigue, it is named fatigue.
If a structure functions as a geometric anchor, it is named accordingly.

The framework therefore prioritizes physical naming over inherited abstraction, with the expectation that terminology should follow mechanism rather than tradition.
